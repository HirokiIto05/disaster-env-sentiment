Proposal

Research Question

Does exposure to natural disasters during impressionable years (high school age) shape the long-term environmental preferences of U.S. legislators?

This project studies whether early-life disaster exposure influences:

  1. The environmental sentiment of legislators’ speech in the Congressional Record
  2. Their revealed environmental policy stance (LCV score)

Hypotheses

  1. Legislators who experienced more severe disaster exposure during high school years use more pro-environment language in congressional speech.
  2. Legislators with higher disaster exposure during high school years have higher LCV scores.
  3. Effects are stronger for exposure occurring during impressionable ages than for non-impressionable periods.

Motivation and # Research Gap

Recent work suggests that natural disaster experienced early-life can affect preferences in later life (\(\ref{@falco_natural_2023}\)). Additionally, individual experiences during formative age affects on a variety of political attitudes and behaviors

However, less is known about whether these effects persist among political elites and influence national policy positions over a long horizon.

If disaster experience during youth leaves a durable imprint, this can help explain heterogeneity in environmental policymaking even within the same party and institutional setting.

The reason that I will analyze sentiment and voting records is that environmental voting results does not always reflect their behavior (\(\ref{@wynes_frequent_2022}\)). The gap between them provides the conprehensive understanding of the effect of early-life disaster exposure on environmental preferences and behavior.

Most Relevant Literature

Environmental preferences and natural disasters
  1. Falco, C., & Corbi, R. (2023), Economics Letters
    Natural disasters and preferences for the environment: Evidence from the impressionable years.
    Key insight: disaster exposure in impressionable years is associated with pro-environment preferences later in life.

Data:Survey data from the US General Social Survey (GSS) and World Values Survey (WVS) matched to disaster records. They found that those who experienced during 18-25 years old are likely to have more pro-environment preferences, and others are not.

  1. Elliott, R. J. R., et al. (2023), Journal of the Association of Environmental and Resource Economists
    Climate-related natural disasters and voting behavior: Evidence from environmental legislation in the U.S. Senate.
    Key insight: climate-related disasters affect legislative behavior on environmental policy.

Method

This study combines individual-level panel construction with text-based sentiment outcomes and policy score outcomes, linking early-life county exposure to later political behavior.

Wild bootstrap standard errors will be used to account for clustering at the county level in disaster exposure.(\(\ref{@falco_natural_2023}\))

Robustness Checks(tentative ideas)

  1. I will reassign the treatment(exposure to disasters) that occurred during the university period and compare the results. Alternatively, identify a time period during which the politician’s location can be determined, and assign the treatment to that period.

  2. Placebo test for differnent roll call voting outcomes that are not related to the environment, such as economic policy votes. (\(\ref{@}\))

Data

Sample

Politicians who was born after 1946 because disaster data is available from 1953 and high school years are typically between ages 14-18. The total number of legislators with high school description on official bioguide is 885 out of 1335 (0.663). The final sample size will depend on the success of matching high school location within the US to disaster data, but I expect to have at least 800 legislators in the final analysis.

Dependent Variables

  1. Congressional speech sentiment (1971-2023) (Progress) Source: USCR Congressional Record API and related text corpora
    • https://data.stanford.edu/congress_text
    • https://github.com/unitedstates/congressional-record/
  2. LCV score (1971-2023) (Done) Source: League of Conservation Voters scorecards and Harvard Dataverse
    • https://scorecard.lcv.org/
    • https://dataverse.harvard.edu/dataset.xhtml?persistentId=doi:10.7910/DVN/GCGJJA
  3. ClimateBERT model Source: ClimateBert: A Pretrained Language Model for Climate-Related Text. (2022) https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.12010 https://www.chatclimate.ai/climatebert

Independent Variable

  1. Disaster exposure during high school years (Progress) Source:
    1. FEMA Disaster Declarations Summary (county-level, 1953-present) https://www.fema.gov/about/openfema/data-sets
    2. Biography data https://bioguide.congress.gov/
    3. High school location(County-level) https://nces.ed.gov/programs/edge/geographic/schoollocations

Scrape biographical text to identify high school location and match to county-level disaster data

Controls

  1. Party affiliation (Democrat / Republican / Independent)
  2. State-level economic indicators (for example, unemployment rate, GDP per capita)
  3. Legislator demographics (for example, age, gender, education)
  4. Additional political controls from Harvard Dataverse
    • https://dataverse.harvard.edu/dataset.xhtml?persistentId=doi:10.7910/DVN/R1PCY6

Expected Results and Contribution

Expected findings are positive and persistent effects of impressionable-year disaster exposure on both rhetoric and policy behavior.

Main contributions:

  1. Evidence on long-run preference formation among political elites
  2. A unified framework connecting early-life shocks, speech, and policy scores
  3. New panel data linkage between biography, schooling geography, and disaster history
