Annotation Guideline

Environmental Policy Stance Classification Congressional Floor Speeches — 108th–112th Congress (2003–2012)

1. Overview

Annotation proceeds in two steps:

Step 1 — Relevance check: Is this segment about one of the six policy categories? Step 2 — Stance classification: If yes, assign one of three labels.

Label Short Definition Core Criterion
POSITIVE Pro-environment The speaker supports environmental regulation or clean energy policy, promotes renewable energy, or accepts climate/pollution science as real and worrying.
NEGATIVE Anti-environment The speaker opposes environmental regulation, promotes fossil fuel development or continuation, or questions, denies, or minimizes the scientific evidence on climate change or pollution.
NEUTRAL No clear stance The speaker describes policy content, reports facts, or takes no discernible position.

Labels are assigned on the basis of speech content alone. Two independent axes determine the label:

  1. Policy stance — does the speaker support or oppose environmental regulation? Does the speaker promote clean energy or fossil fuel development?
  2. Science stance — does the speaker treat climate change or pollution as established fact and express concern, or cast doubt on the science?

Either axis is sufficient to assign POSITIVE or NEGATIVE. The labels reflect what the speaker says, not a moral judgment about environmentalism.

2. Research Context

The texts are speech segments extracted from the U.S. Congressional Record. Each segment consists of a keyword-matched target sentence plus up to three sentences before and after it (a 7-sentence window). Segments are often incomplete and may be cut off mid-argument.

The annotation covers six policy categories selected for their salience in congressional debate and their clear potential for POSITIVE/NEGATIVE stance expression:

Category Core Content
Clean Air Air pollution; Clean Air Act; EPA rules; emission standards; tailpipe limits; CAFE standards
Clean Energy Renewable energy; energy efficiency; electric vehicles; clean electricity
Clean Water Water quality; Clean Water Act; coal ash; mine drainage
Climate Change Global warming; greenhouse gas; carbon policy; Paris Agreement
Dirty Energy Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas); harmful subsidies; tar sands
Wildlife Endangered Species Act; habitat protection; fisheries; migratory birds

Other environmental topics (Toxics, Public Lands, Agriculture, etc.) are outside scope.

3. Step 1: Relevance

3.1 Assign YES if the segment:

  • Discusses a specific law, regulation, or agency action (Clean Air Act, EPA rulemakings, cap-and-trade, Clean Power Plan, etc.)
  • Takes a position — supportive or critical — on an environmental or energy policy
  • Frames fossil fuels, renewable energy, or emissions in an evaluative or policy context
  • Promotes or advocates for fossil fuel development, extraction, or infrastructure (coal, oil, natural gas, tar sands, LNG, pipelines, etc.) regardless of the framing used
  • Uses environmental language to justify or oppose a spending or appropriations decision
  • Argues economic harm (cost, job loss, price increase) and names an environmental regulation as the cause, or grounds the harm in climate science or environmental degradation
  • References climate science facts (temperature projections, sea level rise, etc.) with any evaluative framing — concern, urgency, or skepticism
  • Addresses conservation programs, habitat protection, or wildlife management in a policy context (ESA listings, species recovery, fisheries regulation)

3.2 Assign NO if the segment:

  • Mentions a keyword incidentally with no policy position (e.g., “natural gas” pricing in an unrelated economic speech)
  • Is procedural floor speech or an administrative document with no stance on policy
  • Discusses energy prices, commodity costs, or job losses with no connection to environmental regulation or scientific fact
  • Concerns a foreign country’s energy or climate policy without connection to U.S. legislation
  • Concerns topics outside the six categories: nuclear energy/waste, metal mining, food safety, gun regulation, natural disaster response without policy framing
  • Introduces an individual by describing their background or qualifications without expressing approval of their environmental work — if the speaker actively praises the individual’s contributions to environmental protection or conservation, assign YES and evaluate stance accordingly

4. Step 2: Stance Classification

Apply only to segments with Relevance: YES.

4.1 POSITIVE — Pro-Environment

Assign POSITIVE when the speaker:

  • Explicitly supports a specific environmental regulation or regulatory agency
  • Argues that environmental regulations produce health, economic, or ecological benefits
  • Criticizes efforts to weaken, block, or repeal environmental rules
  • Calls for stricter or new environmental regulations
  • Supports or promotes renewable energy (solar, wind, etc.) in an energy or environmental policy context
  • Supports clean energy policy explicitly linked to climate, pollution, or emission goals
  • Supports ESA listings, habitat protection, or wildlife recovery programs
  • Treats climate change, air pollution, or water contamination as established scientific fact and expresses concern or urgency about it

4.2 NEGATIVE — Anti-Environment

Assign NEGATIVE when the speaker:

  • Explicitly opposes a specific environmental regulation or regulatory agency
  • Argues that regulations impose excessive economic costs, raise energy prices, or destroy jobs — when regulation is named as the cause
  • Supports legislation that weakens, delays, or repeals environmental rules
  • Frames environmental regulation as a barrier to energy production or economic growth
  • Promotes fossil fuel development, extraction, or infrastructure (coal mining, oil drilling, gas pipelines, etc.) as a primary energy strategy
  • Questions, denies, or minimizes climate science — citing scientific uncertainty, attacking the IPCC, framing climate change as unproven, or treating it as a matter of opinion rather than fact
  • Invokes “energy independence” or “energy security” to justify expanding fossil fuel production or opposing clean energy mandates

4.3 NEUTRAL — No Clear Stance

Assign NEUTRAL when the speaker:

  • Describes the procedural content of a bill without expressing approval or disapproval
  • Reports scientific findings in a purely descriptive manner, with no evaluative tone — evident concern or distress about environmental harm is sufficient for POSITIVE
  • Discusses regulatory administration or liability in a purely informational manner
  • Presents both sides without indicating a preference

5. Coded Language Reference

Congressional rhetoric frequently signals position without explicit evaluation. Common patterns:

Phrase / Frame Tone Note
“job-killing regulations” / “regulatory burden / overreach” NEGATIVE
“regulations raise energy / electricity prices” NEGATIVE Only when a regulation is named as cause
“clean coal” (without skepticism) NEGATIVE Fossil fuel continuation frame
“clean coal” + CCS as primary ask (rare) NEUTRAL/POSITIVE Context-dependent
“all of the above” energy policy NEGATIVE Usually fossil fuel continuation; verify context
“scientists disagree” / “global warming is a religion” NEGATIVE Climate science denial
“energy independence” → domestic drilling / oppose mandates NEGATIVE Follows the proposed remedy, not the phrase
“energy independence” → renewables / efficiency POSITIVE
“carbon tax” (criticizing it) NEGATIVE
“carbon tax” (supporting it) POSITIVE
“protecting future generations” POSITIVE
ESA as “job-killer” / “barrier to development” NEGATIVE Follows the property-rights / extraction frame
“voluntary conservation” (opposing mandates) NEGATIVE Opposes regulatory wildlife protection
